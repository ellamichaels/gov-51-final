Legal Context - New York

Non-Custodial Promise to Appear Instead of Custodial Arrest

  * Citation in Lieu of Arrest

    * Can citations be issued as an alternative to custodial arrest for criminal
      offenses?

      - Yes. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 150.20(1) (2013)]

    * Offenses eligible for citations instead of arrest:

      - Any offense. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 150.20(1) (2013)]

    * Circumstances under which citations shall not be issued:

      - Arrestee has been accused of a class A, B, C, or D felony. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 150.20(1)]
      - Arrestee has been accused of a class E felony sex offense or escaping
        custody. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 150.20(1)]
      - Arrestee is accused of a violation of a protective order, felony against
        a family or household member, or misdemeanor family offense. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 140.10(4)]

    * Are citations presumptive for eligible offenses?

      - Yes, issuing an appearance ticket is mandatory for the offense of
        unlawful possession of marihuana, subject to specific conditions. [N.Y.
        Crim. Proc. Law § 150.75 (2013)]

    * Authorities who may issue citations:

      - Any law enforcement officer. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 150.20(1) (2013)]
      - Other authorized personnel. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 150.20(3) (2013)]

  * Sentencing Options

    * Legal status of marijuana:

      - Possession of amount less than 25 grams is decriminalized. [N.Y. Penal
        Law § 221.05 (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 221.10 (2013)]

  * Clearing a Criminal Record

    * Circumstances under which an arrest record may be cleared (qualification
      requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of the criteria):

      - All pending actions or proceedings were resolved in favor of the
        petitioner. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.50(1) (2013)]

  * Age & Charging

    * Minimum age at which an individual can be prosecuted as an adult:

      - 13. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 1.20(42) (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 10.00(18)
        (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 30.00(2) (2013)]

    * Age at which an individual can no longer be considered a juvenile:

      - 16. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 720.10(1)(2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 30.00(1)
        (2013),N.Y.  Fam. Ct. Act § 301.2(1) (2013)]

Cases Rejected for Prosecution

  * Clearing a Criminal Record

    * Circumstances under which an arrest record may be cleared (qualification
      requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of the criteria):

      - All pending actions or proceedings were resolved in favor of the
        petitioner. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.50(1) (2013)]

    * Circumstances under which a conviction record may be cleared
      (qualification requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of
      the criteria):

      - The conviction was for an eligible controlled substance offense, offense
        relating to marijuana, or other “specified offense” as defined by
        statute. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58(1) (2013)]
      - The individual successfully completed a judicially sanctioned drug
        treatment program and has completed the sentence imposed. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 160.58(1) (2013)]
      - A petition or request has been filed and accepted. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law
        § 160.58(3) (2013)]
      - The petitioner has never had a criminal record expunged. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 160.58(3) (2013)]
      - The petitioner is not involved in any pending criminal procedures. [N.Y.
        Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58(7) (2013)]

    * Circumstances under which pardons may be granted:

      - The offense for which the pardon is being sought is not treason, nor is
        it a case of impeachment. [N.Y. Exec. Law § 15 (2013)]
      - Approval from the governor. [N.Y. Exec. Law § 15 (2013)]

    * Do expunged records appear in public record requests?

      - The state does not expunge records, rather, they only authorize sealing.
        [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58 (2013)]
      - Criminal records that have been sealed should not appear in public
        record requests. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58 (2013)]

  * Other collateral consequences

    * Other collateral consequences besides the suspension or revocation of a
      driver's license that may result from a conviction:

      - Public housing may be denied to those convicted of a criminal offense
        relating to the sale or distribution of controlled substances. [N.Y.
        Comp. Codes R. & Regs. tit. 9, § 1627-7.2(d) (2013)]
      - Loss of voting rights for individuals convicted of a felony until all
        periods of incarceration, probation, and parole are completed. [N.Y.
        Elec. Law § 5-106(2)-(4) (2013)]
      - Firearm restrictions. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 530.14(1)-(7) (2013),N.Y.
        Penal Law § 400.00(1),(11) (2013)]
      - Denial of the opportunity to adopt. [N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. tit.
        18, § 421.27(d) (2013)]
      - Termination of parental rights. [N.Y. Soc. Serv. Law § 384-b(4)(e),(8)
        (2013)]

  * Age & Charging

    * Minimum age at which an individual can be prosecuted as an adult:

      - 13. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 1.20(42) (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 10.00(18)
        (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 30.00(2) (2013)]

Nonviolent Misdemeanor Cases with Nonmonetary Release (ROR)

  * Pretrial Release Eligibility

    * Offenses presumptively denied or ineligible for pretrial release:

      - None, with caveats. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 530.40(2) (2013)]
      - Local criminal court may not grant pretrial release when the defendant
        is charged with a class A felony or where it appears the defendant has
        two previous felony convictions. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 530.20(2)(a)
        (2013)]
      - Superior court has full discretion in granting pretrial release in all
        felony cases, except where person being charged has prior conviction for
        a class A felony, or class B or C felony against a person less than 18
        years old. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 530.40(3) (2013)]

    * Does state law require the use of a risk assessment tool in deciding
      pretrial release?

      - Information not located in statutes.

  * Pretrial Release Conditions

    * Does state law ban the use of cash bail as a condition for pretrial
      release for certain offenses?

      - No. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 520.10(1)(a) (2013)]

    * Non-financial conditions of pretrial release authorized by state law:

      - Promise to appear in court (release on own recognizance). [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 510.10 (2013)]
      - Restrictions on contact with victims or witnesses. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law
        § 530.12(1)(a) (2013)]
      - Restrictions on possession and use of firearms or other weapons. [N.Y.
        Crim. Proc. Law § 530.14 (2013)]
      - Submitting to drug or alcohol testing. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law §
        216.05(5),(6) (2013)]
      - Participation in a drug or alcohol treatment program. [N.Y. Crim. Proc.
        Law § 216.05 (2013)]
      - Refraining from any criminal activity. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 216.05(5)
        (2013)]

  * Pretrial Detention

    * Are law enforcement officials permitted to make a pretrial release
      decision when a bail hearing cannot be promptly held?

      - Yes. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 140.20(2),(4) (2013)]

    * Offenses that require a hearing to deny pretrial release:

      - Class A, B, C or D felonies. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 140.20(2) (2013)]
      - Other class E felonies relating to sex offenses and escaping custody, as
        delineated by statute. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 140.20(2) (2013)]

    * If a hearing is not required, who is authorized to make pretrial release
      decisions?

      - NA.

  * Pretrial Services

    * Does state law require that jurisdictions have a pretrial services office?

      - No, however, legislation has since been passed, becoming effective
        January 1, 2020, requiring each county to have at least one pretrial
        service agency. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 510.45 (2018)]

  * Indigent Defense Systems

    * Individual or office responsible for determining indigency:

      - The court. [Beisheim, 34 A.D.2d 329 at 333]

  * Age & Charging

    * Minimum age at which an individual can be prosecuted as an adult:

      - 13. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 1.20(42) (2013),N.Y. Penal Law § 10.00(18)

Pretrial Diversion of Nonviolent Misdemeanors

  * Pretrial Diversion

    * Individuals responsible for pretrial diversion admission decisions (may be
      program-specific):

      - The court. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 216.05(4) (2013)]

    * Types of pretrial diversion authorized by state law:

      - Substance abuse treatment programs. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 216.05
        (2013)]

    * Types of defendants eligible for pretrial diversion (program eligibility
      may be satisfied by different combinations of criteria):

      - Defendants charged with a class B, C, D, or E felony offense relating to
        controlled substances or involving marihuana. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law §
        216.00(1) (2013)]

    * Offenses eligible for pretrial diversion:

      - Class B, C, D and E felony controlled substance and marihuana offenses.
        [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 216.00(1) (2013)]

    * Offenses ineligible for pretrial diversion:

      - None, however, having a contemporaneous pending charge of violent felony
        offense as defined by law or an offense for which merit time allowance
        is not available shall render defendant ineligible for pretrial
        diversion. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 216.00(1) (2013)]

      defendants for admission and participation?

        minimum or aggregate minimum period of the sentence, reduced by any
        merit time accrued, before being eligible for release. [N.Y. Penal Law §
        70.40(1)(a)(i) (2013)]
      - Persons serving one or more determinate sentence must serve the whole
        sentence before being eligible for release. [N.Y. Penal Law §
        70.40(1)(a)(ii) (2013)]
      - Persons serving one or more indeterminate sentence and one or more
        determinate sentence, which run concurrently, may become eligible for
        release after the expiration of the minimum term of the indeterminate
        sentence or expiration of six-sevenths of the determinate sentence,
        whichever is later. [N.Y. Penal Law § 70.40(1)(a)(iii) (2013)]
      - Persons serving one or more indeterminate sentence and one or more
        determinate sentence, which run consecutively, may become eligible for
        release after the expiration of the sum of the minimum term of the
        indeterminate sentence and six-sevenths of the determinate sentence.
        [N.Y. Penal Law § 70.40(1)(a)(iv) (2013)]
      - Persons serving one or more indeterminate or determinate sentence may
        request conditional release upon accruing good time credit equaling the
        unserved portion of their sentence. [N.Y. Penal Law § 70.40(1)(b)
        (2013)]

  * Employment

    * Does state law restrict employment application questions regarding prior
      arrests or convictions?

      - Yes. [N.Y. Exec. Law § 296(16) (2013)]

    * Aside from health care and law enforcement, are there other licensed
      professions that can exclude persons with arrest or conviction records?

      - Law enforcement. [N.Y. Correct. Law § 752 (2013),N.Y. Correct. Law §
        750(5) (2013)]

    * Circumstances under which employers may use criminal records when making
      employment decisions:

      - Record inquiries relate reasonably to the job duties. [N.Y. Correct. Law
        § 752(1) (2013)]
      - When employment poses unreasonable risk to the public. [N.Y. Correct.
        Law § 752(2) (2013)]

  * Public Assistance

    * Does the state participate in the federal TANF and food stamps ban for
      individuals with felony drug convictions?

      - No.

  * Clearing a Criminal Record

    * Circumstances under which a conviction record may be cleared
      (qualification requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of
      the criteria):

      - The conviction was for an eligible controlled substance offense, offense
        relating to marijuana, or other “specified offense” as defined by
        statute. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58(1) (2013)]
      - The individual successfully completed a judicially sanctioned drug
        treatment program and has completed the sentence imposed. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 160.58(1) (2013)]
      - A petition or request has been filed and accepted. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law
        § 160.58(3) (2013)]
      - The petitioner has never had a criminal record expunged. [N.Y. Crim.
        Proc. Law § 160.58(3) (2013)]
      - The petitioner is not involved in any pending criminal procedures. [N.Y.
        Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58(7) (2013)]

    * Circumstances under which pardons may be granted:

      - The offense for which the pardon is being sought is not treason, nor is
        it a case of impeachment. [N.Y. Exec. Law § 15 (2013)]
      - Approval from the governor. [N.Y. Exec. Law § 15 (2013)]

    * Do expunged records appear in public record requests?

      - The state does not expunge records, rather, they only authorize sealing.
        [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58 (2013)]
      - Criminal records that have been sealed should not appear in public
        record requests. [N.Y. Crim. Proc. Law § 160.58 (2013)]
