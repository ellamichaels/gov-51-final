Legal Context - Connecticut

Non-Custodial Promise to Appear Instead of Custodial Arrest

  * Citation in Lieu of Arrest

    * Can citations be issued as an alternative to custodial arrest for criminal
      offenses?

      - Yes. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-1h (2015)]

    * Offenses eligible for citations instead of arrest:

      - All misdemeanors. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-1h (2015)]
      - Any offense for which the penalty is imprisonment for up to one year or
        a fine up to $1,000, or both. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-1h (2015)]

    * Circumstances under which citations shall not be issued:

      - Instances in which a crime of family violence occurred. [Conn. Gen.
        Stat. § 46b-38b(a) (2015)]

    * Are citations presumptive for eligible offenses?

      - No. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-1h (2015)]

    * Authorities who may issue citations:

      - The arresting officer. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-1h (2015)]

  * Sentencing Options

    * Legal status of marijuana:

      - Possession of an amount less than 1/2 oz. is decriminalized. [Conn. Gen.
        Stat. § 21a-279a(a) (2015)]

  * Clearing a Criminal Record

    * Circumstances under which an arrest record may be cleared (qualification
      requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of the criteria):

      - A petition or request has been filed and accepted. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142a (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142d (2015)]
      - The record contains a decriminalized offense. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142d (2015)]
      - The defendant is found not guilty. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a)
        (2015)]
      - The case was dismissed with prejudice. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a)
        (2015)]
      - Individuals convicted of prostitution that was the result of being a
        victim of human trafficking may petition the court to have their record
        expunged. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-95c (2015)]
      - After a period of time has passed. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(c)
        (2015)]
      - The individual has been pardoned. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(d) (2015)]
      - For cases involving multiple offenses, all offenses included in the
        record must be eligible for erasure; however, this restriction does not
        apply to electronic records of the case. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(g)
        (2015)]
      - Individuals convicted of prostitution that was the result of being a
        victim of human trafficking may petition the court to have their record
        expunged. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-95c (2015)]

  * Age & Charging

    * Minimum age at which an individual can be prosecuted as an adult:

      - 15. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-127(a)(1),(b)(1) (2015)]

    * Age at which an individual can no longer be considered a juvenile:

      - 18. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-120 (2015)]

Cases Rejected for Prosecution

  * Clearing a Criminal Record

    * Circumstances under which an arrest record may be cleared (qualification
      requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of the criteria):

      - A petition or request has been filed and accepted. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142a (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142d (2015)]
      - The record contains a decriminalized offense. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142d (2015)]
      - The defendant is found not guilty. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a)
        (2015)]
      - The case was dismissed with prejudice. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a)
        (2015)]
      - Individuals convicted of prostitution that was the result of being a
        victim of human trafficking may petition the court to have their record
        expunged. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-95c (2015)]
      - After a period of time has passed. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(c)
        (2015)]
      - The individual has been pardoned. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(d) (2015)]
      - For cases involving multiple offenses, all offenses included in the
        record must be eligible for erasure; however, this restriction does not
        apply to electronic records of the case. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(g)
        (2015)]
      - Individuals convicted of prostitution that was the result of being a
        victim of human trafficking may petition the court to have their record
        expunged. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-95c (2015)]

    * Circumstances under which a conviction record may be cleared
      (qualification requirements may be satisfied by different combinations of
      the criteria):

      - A petition or request has been filed and accepted. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142a (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142d (2015)]
      - The record contains a decriminalized offense. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142d (2015)]
      - The defendant is found not guilty. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a)
        (2015)]
      - The charges against the defendant were dismissed. [Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-142a(a) (2015)]
      - Individuals convicted of prostitution that was the result of being a
        victim of human trafficking may petition the court to have their record
        expunged. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(a) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-95c (2015)]
      - Thirteen months have elapsed since the case was nolled or continued by
        prosecutor. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(c) (2015)]
      - The individual has been pardoned. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(d) (2015)]
      - For cases involving multiple offenses, all offenses included in the
        record must be eligible for erasure; however, this restriction does not
        apply to electronic records of the case. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142a(g)
        (2015)]

    * Circumstances under which pardons may be granted:

      - An application or petition has been submitted and accepted. [Conn. Gen.
        Stat. § 54-130a (2015)]
      - Three years have elapsed since the conviction for a misdemeanor or
        violation. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-130a (2015)]
      - Five years have elapsed since the conviction of a felony. [Conn. Gen.
        Stat. § 54-130a (2015)]

    * Do expunged records appear in public record requests?

      - No. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-142c(a) (2015)]

  * Other collateral consequences

    * Other collateral consequences besides the suspension or revocation of a
      driver's license that may result from a conviction:

      - Denial of public housing assistance. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 8-45a (2015)]
      - Loss of voting rights for individuals convicted of a felony until all
        periods of incarceration, probation, and parole are completed. [Conn.
        Gen. Stat. § 9-46(a) (2015)]
      - Firearm restrictions. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53a-217 (2015)]
      - Adoption restrictions. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 17a-114 (2015)]
      - Termination of parental rights. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 45a-717(g) (2015)]

  * Age & Charging

    * Minimum age at which an individual can be prosecuted as an adult:

      - 15. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-127(a)(1),(b)(1) (2015)]

    * Age at which an individual can no longer be considered a juvenile:

      - 18. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-120 (2015)]

Pretrial Diversion of Nonviolent Misdemeanors

  * Pretrial Diversion

    * Individuals responsible for pretrial diversion admission decisions (may be
      program-specific):

      - The court. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e(b) (2015)]

    * Types of pretrial diversion authorized by state law:

      - Accelerated pretrial rehabilitation program. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e
        (2015)]
      - Pretrial drug education and community service program. [Conn. Gen. Stat.
        § 54-56i (2015)]
      - Veterans programs. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56l (2015)]
      - Mental health programs. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56l (2015)]
      - Family violence education program. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-38c (2015)]
      - First-time weapons offender program. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 29-33(h)
        (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 29-37a(i) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-202l(e)
        (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 53-202w(g) (2015)]

    * Types of defendants eligible for pretrial diversion (program eligibility
      may be satisfied by different combinations of criteria):

      - Defendants with no prior convictions. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]
      - Defendants with no prior felony convictions. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e
        (2015)]
      - Veterans who suffer from a military-related mental illness, a traumatic
        brain injury, or a substance abuse problem. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56l
        (2015)]
      - Defendants not previously convicted of a serious violent offense. [Conn.
        Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]

    * Offenses eligible for pretrial diversion:

      - Crimes or violations that are not of a serious nature. [Conn. Gen. Stat.
        § 54-56e (2015)]

    * Offenses ineligible for pretrial diversion:

      - Offenses related to motor vehicles. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]
      - Class A felonies. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]
      - Certain class B felonies. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]
      - Assault in the second degree. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]
      - Voting violations. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e (2015)]

    * Does state law allow pretrial diversion programs to charge a fee to
      defendants for admission and participation?

      - Yes. [Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46b-38c(i) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e(b)
        (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e(d) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56e(e)
        (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56g(a)(1) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-56i(b) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 54-56i(g) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        54-56i(i) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. § 17a-696(e) (2015),Conn. Gen. Stat. §
        53a-39c(b) (2015)]
